01 · The brief

A brand outgrew its spreadsheet,
and the Monday meeting that propped it up.

The DTC brand sells personal-care across DTC web, Amazon US/CA, a wholesale portal, and three retail consignment partners. Each channel had its own attribution. Each meeting started with reconciliation. The founder had stopped trusting her own ROAS number.

  • Four tools, four versions of "revenue this week" — usually off by 12–18%.
  • Inventory was reactively re-ordered after stockouts, costing about 6 days of lost sales per quarter.
  • Klaviyo flows triggered on stale customer state — repeat-buyer emails landed on cancelled accounts.
  • The team avoided an off-the-shelf ERP because none of them mapped to consignment + Amazon FBA + Shopify cleanly.

"I needed one number I could trust by Tuesday. We were running a brand on four dashboards and a spreadsheet that broke every time anyone touched it."

— Founder · NA e-commerce brand
02 · What we shipped

A small, opinionated ERP
built around how this brand actually sells.

A Next.js operations app on top of Postgres, with ClickHouse for analytics. Channel adapters pull Shopify, Amazon FBA, Klaviyo, Meta, and the consignment portal into a normalized order ledger. One product. One customer. One number for ROAS, computed identically across all channels.

Why this stack survived production

  • A canonical order ledger means "revenue" is computed once. Every report reads from the same number.
  • ClickHouse handles cohort + attribution queries that Shopify Analytics quietly down-samples.
  • Klaviyo + Meta receive enriched events back — so audiences finally know who an LTV-positive customer is.
  • Inventory model is forward-looking: it sees Amazon FBA inbound shipments, consignment pickups, and Shopify burn rate as one curve.
  • Founder + ops manager both have role-scoped views — no "let me check with finance" hand-offs.
