01 · The brief

Two hundred forty trucks,
and a whiteboard.

Dispatch was a 6:00am ritual: a senior dispatcher with 14 years of Riyadh-by-heart knowledge, drawing routes on a magnetic board. It worked — until the operator won three retail contracts in one quarter and the board ran out of squares.

  • Routes were assembled in 90 minutes every morning. Anything later than 6:30am started bleeding the day.
  • Vehicle utilization was 61% — every fleet has a long tail of half-empty runs.
  • Two prior SaaS routing tools were rejected: they couldn't handle prayer-time stops, Ramadan schedules, or KSA address ambiguity.
  • The COO had a board deadline: prove the route engine pays for itself in one quarter, or kill the project.

"We didn't want optimization theater. We wanted something Ahmed could look at, override when his gut said so, and trust the rest of the time."

— COO · KSA last-mile operator
02 · What we shipped

A daily route plan, in 90 seconds,
with the dispatcher in the loop.

A two-layer system: OR-Tools handles the deterministic optimization (VRPTW with time windows, vehicle skills, capacity), while a learned re-ranker scores routes by what actually happened on Riyadh streets last week — traffic, prayer-time clusters, building access realities. Ahmed sees the plan with explanations, drags edits, and the system learns from his overrides.

Why this stack survived production

  • OR-Tools VRPTW handles the hard constraints (capacity, time windows, driver skills) — deterministic, explainable.
  • The re-ranker layer learned from 60 days of actual driver telemetry — that's where the "knows the city" feel comes from.
  • Prayer-time clusters are first-class: routes naturally collapse delivery windows around five-times-daily quiet periods.
  • Address resolution layer for KSA's informal addressing — Google geocoding alone failed on 24% of orders.
  • Override-aware: every dispatcher tweak feeds the next training run. The system gets smarter, the dispatcher stays in charge.
